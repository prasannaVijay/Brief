The problem
Every workday starts scattered across a dozen surfaces — mail, calendar, Slack, GitHub, docs, news — each demanding attention, none showing the whole picture. Brief reads the surfaces you choose, decides what actually matters to you today, and pushes you one short briefing plus the reminders that follow.

Warm-up for the room: which surfaces would you wire up — and what did you drop last week that a brief would've caught?

The thesis

Why an agent, not a dashboard or a cron script?

A dashboard shows you everything and makes you do the triage. The one part that's genuinely hard — deciding what matters to you today — is exactly what a language model is good at.

Everything else in Brief is plumbing around that small judgment. Keep that ratio in mind — it's most of the system.

The loop: let the model decide what to pull, and when it's done.

Tool use + planning. The agent chooses which surfaces to hit, reads results, and stops when it has enough — instead of a fixed script.

And because the model is stateless, we own memory: what's been sent, recent history, config. That's SQLite, not the model. Session 4

Knowledge: for what won't fit in the window.

Embeddings + retrieval. A wiki or a year of notes is too big for context, so we embed it and pull back only the relevant pieces to ground a reminder.

"You said you'd follow up on X" comes from retrieval, not the model's memory. Session 5

Deliver & ship: also not the model.

Push is just an output surface — a reminder written outward instead of read inward (ntfy, email). And Brief ships as a scheduled job: a container or a uvx one-liner that wakes, briefs, sleeps.

The lesson worth stating: agentic ≠ always-on server. It's usually a batch job with durable state. Sessions 3 & 10

The toolkit

Two independent choices: which model, and how you build around it.

Both move fast, and getting either wrong is reversible — as long as you keep them loosely coupled. Let's take them in turn.

Goal for this segment: you could defend a different choice for your own team, not just repeat ours.

Choice 1 · which model snapshot · jul 2026

The frontier is compressed. Pick by fit, not by leaderboard.

Per-token cost, data leaves your walls, some lock-in

Open-weight · Llama 4, DeepSeek, Qwen…

Data stays in-house, cheap at volume, tunable; coding gap has largely closed
You own the serving and ops

No universal best — match model to task, and build so swapping is a config change, not a rewrite. (Names shift monthly; refresh per cohort.)

Choice 2 · how you build snapshot · jul 2026

In 2026 the real question is: do you even need a framework?

Raw SDK + thin layeranthropic / openai sdk
+ transparent, debuggable, minimal deps. – you write the loop.
Focused agent SDKsclaude agent sdk · pydantic ai · langgraph
+ structure for loop & state; native MCP. – a dependency, some lock-in.
Heavyweight frameworkslangchain · llamaindex
+ huge connector library, deep RAG. – abstraction bloat, harder to productionize.
Managed platformsbedrock agentcore · vertex agent builder
+ governance, identity, SLAs. – vendor tenant, less control.

Better native tool-calling + MCP standardizing integrations have pushed many teams down this ladder. MCP sits underneath all of them — it's the protocol, not a framework.

What Brief picks — and why
  • modelClaude — Sonnet for most steps, Opus for the hard reasoning
  • buildRaw Anthropic SDK + a hand-rolled loop — the mechanics stay visible
  • connectMCP-first, with a model-agnostic seam so you can swap in GPT / Gemini / open
  • growAdd a focused library only when a session earns it (e.g. heavy RAG)
We're optimizing for learning, not shipping fastest. You can't judge an abstraction until you've built what it abstracts. For a real production build under deadline, you'd lean harder on an agent SDK or a managed platform — and that's a fine answer.
